2. Laser Cutting

This was the first week we were able to use the machines in the MakerSpace! We used the Epilog Laser Cutter to explore lasering on a variety of materials, creating swatches and then creating a box with a "living hinge."

Learning and Planning

This week the class advanced from the liminal realm of code to the tactile world of machines. This was definitely a big step, there was more room for error and more on the line if we messed up. I made sure to study the pre-work materials thoroughly before attempting anything on the actual machine.

I played around with adobe for a bit, figuring out where everything was.

After studying up on how to work the laser cutter and how to navigate Adobe Illustrator, I decided on the materials for my five swatches. I experimented with ¼ plywood, ⅙ cardboard, ⅛ acrylic, ⅛ plywood, and copy paper.

Swatch making time

I made my template for my swatches on illustrator, a 2 x 2 inch square with rounded corners, using “myriad pro” font.

I started with the ¼ plywood. Because I wanted to make sure I wasn’t going to set my settings too high and damage the laser cutter, I found a material template as a point of reference for determining my vector and raster settings. I sent my image to print and sorted out the Epilog laser cutter settings, both on the machine itself (using the jog and focus options and the leveling tool, setting those) and on the Epilog app settings (adjusting my vector and raster settings so they matched the ones I had put on my swatch).

I started with my settings as such: vector = 10S 100P 100F; raster = 40S 70P. While I was satisfied with the vector results, the text ended up being way too light so I decided to amp up the raster settings to 30S and 80P. This ended up working quite well.

Note the comparison between these two cuts.

Note: the raster speed and power control the depth of the text or image engraved on the surface while the vector is the more slicing and powerful cut, cutting straight through the material.

I moved onto the cardboard, again using a material template as a gauge for my raster and vector settings. I used the same settings I had for my ¼ plywood due to their very similar width.

I ended up printing on the template too because there was unused space.

Next, I cut a piece of blue acrylic. I was nervous for this one especially because I was unfamiliar with the material. I chose my vector and raster settings and tried it out.

It ended up going well and I was happy with the swatch.

Next was the thinner ⅛ plywood. I went through the same process as the other swatches and was happy with the raster and vector settings I had decided on.

For my last swatch, I thought I would try copy paper. I was nervous for this cut too because it was so much thinner than all the other materials I had used previously. I started with my raster and vector settings very low to see how the cut went.

I felt like this cut was too swift and seemed almost close to burning, so I lowered both the raster and vector settings slightly further. My goal was to raster the text onto the paper so it was visible but didn’t cut through the paper. This ended up still being too high.

This is a comparison of my second to my first cut.

I lowered both settings still further and was able to get the result I wanted, the text was visible but didn’t cut through the paper.

After this experimentation with the different facets of laser cutting and adobe, I felt ready to move forward to create my box!

Making of the box

I explored the options for flex boxes on the box plan generator and settled on the “box with living hinge and round corners.” I set the thickness of the box to 3.175 as I was to use ⅛ plywood. Other than that, I stuck with the template, which was a cube shape with rounded corners. I downloaded this image and saved it to the computer and then opened it in adobe to edit it and correct the settings. This is where I added my initials on one side as well.

I then selected the top part of the design, the supporting sides, to try printing on their own. I aligned them to the top left corner as I had done with my swatches and adjusted the raster and vector settings to match my ⅛ plywood swatch.

The cut went well. Here I compare the size and result of the sides of my box to my swatch.

Next, I cut the long connected side to my box. I went through the same process as I did with the sides of the box. Here, I’m showing how I had to change the media size for the file so that it would align correctly and agree with the laser cutter. I also had to make sure to place the image in the top left hand corner.

Time to print!

This is how the side came out.
